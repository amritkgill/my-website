## The Paper

This is a summary and response to a *Journal of Economic Perspectives* article on the 1990s technology boom and productivity growth.

The paper looks at the growth of the US economy during the technology boom of the late 1990s. It talks about the rise of the computer age, calling it the "New Economy," and questions whether the productivity growth during that time really matched up with the big economic booms of the past, like the First Industrial Revolution (1760-1830) and the Second one powered by electricity (1860-1900).

Between 1995 and 1999, the data looked great with output, growth, and productivity all increasing. It almost looked like we were going to be in another golden age, maybe even a Third Industrial Revolution. But the author argues that it was different this time since growth from computers didn't spread throughout the whole economy. In earlier periods, productivity growth was coming from investment and infrastructure that changed everything. Computers started to show diminishing returns, and the impact during the 90s was limited to leisure and parts of manufacturing.

## My Take

The article makes an interesting point, but hindsight really changes how we see it. Reading the article today in 2025, it's obvious the author couldn't have known how far the internet would go and how it would shape globalization, communication, and basically every part of daily life.

I do understand where he is coming from because at the time, the computer industry didn't seem revolutionary like electricity or printing. If the internet stayed small and stuck to manufacturing, I would probably still agree with him. Right now, it is very clear that the influence of the internet went way beyond what anyone ever expected.

## Where I'd Take This Further

I think in today's age, I would frame the research question in terms of AI. Everyone is calling it the next big thing, and it's kind of similar to the reading and how it talks about the internet because right now we don't know how far AI will go. I wonder if it will reshape certain industries or the whole economy.

I know people and companies who specialize in AI are becoming very rich. On the other hand, we constantly hear that AI will take people's jobs and make everyone have a universal basic income. Will it only drive up wealth for a small group of people? Does replacing people count as growth, or is it hurting the economy by cutting wages and spending power?

These are definitely complex questions, especially because it's such a new concept and we just don't know the extent of AI. I think it's worth exploring these with data on which industries use AI, productivity changes, jobs, and unemployment rates. This will help us see how AI is being adopted and the influence especially to see if it's a real turning point or just another hype cycle.
