## The Paper

This is my review of a paper on the causal relationship between financial development and economic growth, using panel data from 109 countries between 1960 and 1994.

The paper discusses the relationship between financial development and economic growth and uses the Geweke decomposition test to test this. It uses panel data and finds that in developing countries, financial development causes economic growth more from increased investments and better use of resources. Industrial countries show a stronger effect of economic growth to finance, but both groups show a two-way relationship too.

## Methods

The methods used in this paper are appropriate because they use better financial data and a newer and more detailed analysis to see the full relationship between financial development and economic growth. Past papers usually only looked at one way relationships and not both directions, unlike this paper.

The Geweke decomposition test is a good method because it lets the author break down the relationship and look at how finance affects economic growth, vice versa, and both happening at the same time. Older tests like the Granger test only let you look at one way relationships and don't measure the overall relationship.

The data used was panel data from 109 countries between 1960 and 1994, which is also better than past papers since they would only analyze a few countries. The data is reliable and relevant since analyzing many countries over a long period of time makes the analysis more powerful.

## Concerns

One potential concern with the analysis is that they have missed some important factors that affect both finance and economic growth like politics or institutions, which could lead to omitted variable bias. There could be other factors influencing both variables at the same time, which could lead to endogeneity. The Geweke decomposition test also assumes the relationship to be linear, and it could be nonlinear.

## Suggestions

One way the paper can be improved is adding more recent data to see if the results still apply today. Including more factors will make the analysis more accurate and we could really see the full impact of the relationship.
