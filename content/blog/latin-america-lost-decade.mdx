## The Paper

This is a summary and response to Arminio Fraga's 2004 article "Latin America Since the 1990s: Rising from the Sickbed?" published in the *Journal of Economic Perspectives*.

The article discusses Latin America's development and uses data per country, like GDP per capita, inflation, school enrollment and life expectancy to show how conditions improved in the 1990s compared to the 1980s. The 1980s were known as the "lost decade" for Latin America because of debt and declining GDP, and many banks and currencies collapsed.

The growth in the 1990s is due to the Washington Consensus, which meant tighter budgets and increased trade, and it brought down inflation and stabilized currencies. People's lives also improved with fewer kids dying young and increased literacy. On the other hand, productivity didn't grow too much and there was still inequality with countries like Argentina and Venezuela continually experiencing crises. For real long term growth, Latin America needs to stick with reforms and build better institutions to avoid going back to populism.

## My Take

Even though growth was uneven, I agree that Latin America was better off in the 1990s compared to the 1980s. The article does a good job pairing data with economic analysis, especially with how it explained the damage populism caused compared to the stability that the reforms brought. Another strength was how it tied social progress to the bigger picture of economic growth. The example they gave of Chile and Mexico's success compared to Argentina and Venezuela's struggle helped make it easier to understand.

One critique is that the article mostly talks about the reforms in Latin America but doesn't connect them to what was happening in the rest of the world. It doesn't go into depth about global trade and new technology which had a strong impact on growth.

## Where I'd Take This Further

I'd want to look at why productivity didn't really improve even though education and health did. Life expectancy and literacy rates increased, which usually translates into faster growth like in East Asia, but it didn't in Latin America. Why didn't these improvements lead to better jobs and a stronger labor market?

I would use data on the types of jobs, rural versus urban areas, and job market changes in the 1990s versus 1980s. If the economy isn't growing even though people are healthier and more educated, then there is something wrong and the region isn't making the most out of its human capital which is contributing to inequality. If this question is explored more in depth, policymakers could create programs that connect people with better opportunities leading to long-term growth.
