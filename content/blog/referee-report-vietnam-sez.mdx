## The Paper

This is my review of a paper on Special Economic Zones in Vietnam, published in the *Journal of Development Economics* (2025).

The main research question that this paper explores is how Special Economic Zones and Foreign Direct Investment affect the labor market in Vietnam. The study uses difference-in-differences and finds that the expansion of Special Economic Zones led to workers moving from unpaid agricultural work to receiving wages at firms. The effects were stronger for women, and workers experienced higher earnings and improved quality of employment.

## Methods

The methods used are appropriate because the study used difference-in-differences to compare areas that received Special Economic Zones to those that didn't, which helped the researchers measure the true effect of them by controlling for differences that don't change over time and general trends happening everywhere.

The data is reliable and relevant because the researchers used very detailed sources that let them make more focused and accurate predictions. They improved on earlier research by using old satellite images from Google Earth to measure how much land was built up inside the Special Economic Zones over time, which was the continuous variable and looked at district level changes. They also used individual data from two national surveys from 2009 to 2019 to study the changes in jobs people had and to analyze differences in gender, education, and rural or urban location. Labor force data from 2013 to 2019 was used to study the changes in wages and job quality as well.

The time period they focused on helped make sure their analysis was strong because this was when Vietnam became more connected to the global market through increased exports and the satellite images were higher quality. The authors only included areas that received Special Economic Zones after 2009 and compared them to areas that didn't have them at the time but planned to later, which controlled for any non-parallel trends.

## Concerns

One potential concern with the analysis is that although they say that the districts were similar pre-treatment, they can show proof with some tests or graphs that show they were actually similar. Also, they briefly mentioned that they accounted for migration but they could do a more detailed analysis on this because it could be that the labor market effects were because of new incoming workers.

## Suggestions

One suggestion for improvement is to explore the different types of Special Economic Zones, such as manufacturing and service, impact workers differently. They could also expand their analysis by examining how housing prices and services around them are affected and how policymakers can use their findings so we can really see the full importance.
