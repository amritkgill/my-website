## The Paper

This is a summary and response to a paper published in the *Journal of Economic Perspectives* on poverty and economic growth in the United States.

The paper explores why the non-elderly poverty rate has barely changed since 1970, even though the U.S. has had strong economic growth. The authors used Current Population Surveys, U.S. Bureau of Census data, and Panel Study of Income Dynamics data to analyze trends associated with poverty and factors like gender, wages, family structure, government programs, immigration, and unemployment.

They find that the main reasons are weak wage growth and rising inequality among workers who are paid lower wages. Increased female labor force participation reduced poverty but was canceled out by the rise in single-parent families. Immigration and government programs had a limited impact on the official poverty rate. The unchanged non-elderly poverty rate is a family dynamic and labor market issue rather than one being directly tied with overall economic growth.

## My Take

I agree with the paper's conclusion that poverty has remained high even though the economy has grown. It's surprising that even with many women entering the workforce, the poverty rate didn't drop, which emphasizes how strong of an impact family dynamics and inequality have on poverty.

I found it interesting that the government programs were only helpful in some ways and didn't have a real impact on the actual poverty rate, which made me think of how the way poverty is measured can change how effective policies are. The paper uses strong data to clearly highlight all the relationships between poverty, labor markets and demographics, making the arguments easy to follow.

One critique is that the research design could have been improved by doing a deeper dive into different subgroups of women, immigrants, and minorities so we can really see who benefits from economic growth and who is left behind. This would also be helpful to policymakers since they could see which groups are more vulnerable and what specific programs to implement.

## Where I'd Take This Further

A valuable research question would be to investigate families that leave poverty and stay out to better understand which policies are most effective in breaking the cycle. While some government assistance programs such as TANF increase income for the poor, they don't significantly impact the poverty rate since they're provided to families way below the poverty line. Since the poverty rate is measured using pretax income, the impact of EITC doesn't really show up, which makes me think that these post-tax programs might not be the best way on their own to help families move out of poverty.

It would be interesting to look at families just below the poverty line to see whether income assistance or education programs can provide the necessary push out of poverty, and what kind of long term support helps them stay out.
