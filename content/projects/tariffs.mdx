## Overview

This capstone investigates whether the 2018 Section 301 tariffs on Chinese imports caused U.S. multinational firms to shift a greater share of profits to foreign jurisdictions. Using a continuous difference-in-differences design with two-way fixed effects, the study exploits cross-industry variation in tariff exposure to identify the causal effect on corporate effective tax rates.

The main finding: tariff-exposed firms saw a significant drop in effective tax rates after 2018, consistent with increased profit shifting to lower-tax countries.

## Data

The final panel contains **14,397 firm-year observations** across **1,674 firms** from 2015 to 2024, drawn from three sources:

- **Bloomberg Terminal**: firm-level annual financials including effective tax rate, revenue, R&D expense, total assets, debt, and capital expenditure
- **SEC EDGAR XBRL**: foreign and domestic pre-tax income from 10-K filings, used to construct the Foreign Profit Share robustness check
- **USITC / Section 301 tariff lists**: industry-level tariff exposure measured at the NAICS 3-digit level, covering 24 goods-producing industries

Tariff exposure ranges from 4% (Furniture) to 21% (Computer and Electronic Products). Service-sector firms drop out by construction since Section 301 tariffs apply only to imported goods.

## Methodology

The main estimating equation is a continuous diff-in-diff with two-way fixed effects:

**ETR_it = B(TariffExposure_i x Post2018_t) + Controls_it + FirmFE_i + YearFE_t + error_it**

- **ETR**: effective tax rate, winsorized at p1/p99
- **TariffExposure**: mean Section 301 tariff increase for the firm's NAICS-3 industry (continuous, 0.04 to 0.21)
- **Post2018**: equals 1 for 2019 onward (first full fiscal year under tariffs)
- **Controls**: log revenue, R&D intensity, leverage, TCJA exposure
- Standard errors clustered at the NAICS-3 level (24 industries), with wild cluster bootstrap for inference

The coefficient B tells us whether firms with more tariff exposure saw a different change in ETR after 2018 compared to firms with less exposure.

## Key Findings

The main coefficient is **-68.0** (p < 0.001, wild bootstrap p = 0.009). For the most exposed industry: Computer and Electronics at 21% tariff: the implied ETR drop is **14.3 percentage points**. For a less exposed industry like Furniture at 4%, the drop is **2.7 percentage points**.

The conservative estimate using tighter winsorization (p5/p95) gives a coefficient of **-24.3**, implying a 5.1 pp drop for Computer and Electronics. Both estimates are economically meaningful: a 5 pp ETR drop means a firm paid roughly $50 million less in taxes per billion of income.

## Event Study

The event study traces the dynamic response of ETR to tariff exposure year by year, with 2017 as the reference year. The pre-period coefficients for 2015 and 2016 are close to zero and not significant, supporting the parallel trends assumption. The effect turns on in 2019: the first full fiscal year under tariffs: and remains negative through 2024.

The 2018 spike makes economic sense: tariff-exposed firms faced sudden cost increases before they had time to restructure their tax planning.

## ETR Distribution

The distribution of effective tax rates shifted leftward after tariffs were imposed. Among tariff-exposed firms, the post-tariff distribution shows a clear shift toward lower ETR values, consistent with increased profit shifting.

## Robustness

The result holds across 12 robustness specifications:

- **Dropping controls**: coefficient barely changes (-66.4 vs -68.0)
- **SIC 1-digit x year FE**: controls for broad sector trends, still significant (-61.2, p = 0.001)
- **NAICS-2 x year FE**: aggressive fixed effects, still significant (-39.6, p = 0.009)
- **Placebo test**: fake 2017 treatment gives wrong-sign coefficient (+85.2, p = 0.107), confirming no pre-existing trend
- **Balanced panel**: restricting to firms present all 10 years gives nearly identical results (-66.2, p < 0.001)
- **COVID interaction**: COVID partially reversed the effect during 2020-2021; excluding those years strengthens the result to -84.9

![Robustness forest plot](/projects/tariffs/robustness_forest_plot.png)

## Alternative Tariff Measures

Four different ways of measuring tariff exposure were tested. Only the tariff **rate** measures matter: the tariff **count** measures do not. This makes economic sense: firms respond to how expensive the tariff is, not how many product lines got hit.

## Interpretation

Tariffs raise costs for exposed firms, and firms look for ways to offset those costs. One mechanism is to shift profits to low-tax jurisdictions: when income is booked abroad, less is taxed at U.S. rates, producing a lower ETR. If firms weren't profit shifting, they'd simply absorb the tariff costs with no change in ETR. The fact that ETR drops specifically for tariff-exposed firms after 2018 is evidence of profit shifting.

The TCJA is not confounding the result: the correlation between tariff exposure and pre-treatment foreign profit share is only 0.03, and adding or removing the TCJA control barely changes the coefficient.

R&D-intensive firms show larger ETR drops, which supports the profit shifting explanation: firms with patents and intellectual property have more tools to relocate profits across jurisdictions.

## Limitations & Next Steps

- **Tariff exposure is measured at the industry level**, not the firm level. This introduces classical attenuation bias, meaning the true effect is likely larger than estimated.
- **ETR outlier sensitivity**: the headline coefficient is inflated by extreme ETR values; the conservative estimate of -24.3 (p5/p95) is more defensible.
- **Next steps** include a leave-one-industry-out test, firm-level heterogeneity analysis by size and R&D intensity, and exploring firm-level import data from Census/Comtrade for more precise tariff exposure measurement.
