## Overview

This paper examines whether compulsory voting laws causally increase voter turnout. Using a country-year panel dataset covering 202 countries from 1945 to 2025, a difference-in-differences design compares changes in turnout before and after the adoption of compulsory voting laws relative to countries that never adopted such laws. The results suggest that compulsory voting increases turnout by approximately 10 percentage points.

## Motivation

Voter turnout is a significant indicator of democratic health. Participation rates remain low in many countries, raising concerns about the legitimacy of democratic systems and balanced political influence. Compulsory voting aims to close the participation gap by requiring all eligible citizens to vote, strengthening democracy through broader participation and representativeness.

## Data

The dependent variable is voter turnout, measured as the percentage of the voting-age population that casts a ballot. The main explanatory variable is a binary indicator for whether a universal compulsory voting law was in effect in that country-year. Voter turnout data comes from the International IDEA Voter Turnout Database, merged with a manually coded dataset on compulsory voting laws constructed from official government records.

The merged dataset includes 2,441 country-year observations. The indicator reflects legal status only and enforcement is not measured, making this an Intention-to-Treat estimate.

## Methodology

### Two-Way Fixed Effects

The main estimating equation is a TWFE model with country fixed effects (capturing time-invariant national characteristics) and year fixed effects (capturing global shocks). Standard errors are clustered at the country level.

```python
import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# Two-way fixed effects with clustered standard errors

    "Q('Voter Turnout') ~ Compulsory + C(cty_fe) + C(year_fe)",
```

### Event Study

An event study model tracks changes in voter turnout in the years surrounding adoption to test the parallel trends assumption. The year immediately before adoption serves as the reference point.

## Key Findings

The coefficient on compulsory voting is **10.18 percentage points** and statistically significant (p = 0.005). Given an average turnout rate of 71%, this corresponds to about a 14% relative increase in participation.

The event study shows turnout remains stable in the years leading up to the law, supporting the parallel trends assumption. After adoption, turnout increases sharply and persists, suggesting the effect is both immediate and lasting.

A pre-treatment balance test confirms that switcher countries (70.3% average turnout) and never-treated countries (70.1%) were nearly identical before adoption, strengthening the causal interpretation.

### Robustness

- Adding country-specific linear time trends: effect is 6.75 p.p.
- Leave-one-out test (dropping one country at a time): effect ranges from 7.75 to 11.88 p.p.
- No single country drives the result.
