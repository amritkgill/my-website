## Overview

This project investigates how macroeconomic conditions drive housing prices in the United States. Specifically, it estimates the effect of the unemployment rate and Consumer Price Index on the House Price Index using Ordinary Least Squares regression on annual data spanning 1975 to 1994, sourced from FRED via Kaggle.

## Research Question

How do the Unemployment Rate and Consumer Price Index affect the House Price Index?

## Methodology

The model regresses the House Price Index on the unemployment rate and CPI. OLS estimation was performed both manually (computing the beta coefficients via the normal equation) and using statsmodels to verify results and obtain inference statistics.

```python
import statsmodels.api as sm

# OLS with unemployment rate and CPI
features = data[['Unemployment_Rate', 'Consumer_Price_Index']]
X = sm.add_constant(features)
y = data['House_Price_Index']

model = sm.OLS(y, X).fit()
print(model.summary())
```

### Manual OLS

As a learning exercise, the coefficients were also derived by hand using the normal equation to verify the library output:

```python
import numpy as np

X_transpose = np.transpose(X)
XtX_inv = np.linalg.inv(np.dot(X_transpose, X))
beta = np.dot(XtX_inv, np.dot(X_transpose, y))
```

## Key Findings

- **Unemployment Rate** coefficient: -3.76: a 1 percentage point increase in unemployment is associated with a 3.76-point decrease in the House Price Index, holding CPI constant.
- **Consumer Price Index** coefficient: 1.02: a 1-point increase in CPI is associated with a 1.02-point increase in the HPI, holding unemployment constant.
- **R-squared: 0.997**: the model explains 99.7% of the variation in house prices.
- Both coefficients are statistically significant (p < 0.001).

## Limitations

The condition number (1,060) suggests potential multicollinearity between the regressors. Mortgage rate and real disposable income were initially considered but omitted due to lack of statistical significance within this dataset, despite being theoretically relevant to housing markets. A larger dataset or alternative specification could better capture their influence.
